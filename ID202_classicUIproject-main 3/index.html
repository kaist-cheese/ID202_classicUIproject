2022-2025-

                    
Log Entry: The KMLA Atoll. These three years were defined by the primal act of survival. One hundred fifty souls, marooned.

                    
Escape—the pursuit of a 'better isle'—demanded the construction of vessels. Yet, far from a collective endeavor, it devolved into a zero-sum game of trampling others to craft a more resilient ship. Such was the nature of this place: relentlessly demanding, profoundly arduous.

                    
I, of course, refused the prescribed vessel. I built my own, guided by my own convictions and talents.

                    
Precious bonds were forged, yet they could not share my passage. That was the unwritten law of the atoll.

                    
Log Entry: The Embarkation. The day I met the sea, the weight of three years' toil felt like the tide itself. The voyage was no gentle passage. Snow fell, rains lashed, and thunder articulated the chaos.

                    
Still, cessation was not an option. I pressed onward. I navigated past the specters of companions who could not complete this journey... I made landfall on a new island, designated 'KAIST'.

                    
Log Entry: The KAIST Archipelago. I had anticipated a different topography of existence. A new environment, a new life. I expected tranquility.

                    
But the inhabitants here are merely inebriated with the relief of 'arrival' and the narcotic of 'achievement.' The essence remains unchanged.

                    
Here, too, survival is the fundamental imperative. Here, too, exodus is the eventual necessity.

                    
At times, I find myself nostalgic for the KMLA atoll. At least there, a rigid cartography of 'do's' and 'don'ts' existed. Here, even that rudimentary framework is absent.

                    
Log Entry: The Present. And so, I build again. A vessel of unprecedented scale. The elder inhabitants offer no wisdom; only I can be the architect of this design.

                    
Most who arrived with me are still paralyzed by indecision, contemplating the very nature of their craft—a simple sailboat, a formidable warship, perhaps a humble fishing boat. They hesitate.

                    
I... I know the form of my vessel. And yet... The psychic weight is immense. It is all simply too much.

                    
I long for egress. To return to the origin point, the 'terra firma' whence I came. But there is no time for such pilgrimages. I am consumed by the ornamentation of my ship, the calibration of its engine for a farther, truer voyage.

                    
...That is my one desire.
